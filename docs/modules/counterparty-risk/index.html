Introduction to Counterparty Credit Risk

What is Counterparty Credit Risk?

Counterparty credit risk (CCR) is the risk that a counterparty to a financial contract will default before the final settlement of the transaction's cash flows. Unlike traditional credit risk, CCR creates a bilateral risk profile because the market value of the transaction can be positive or negative for either counterparty at different points in time.

Counterparty credit risk is particularly relevant for:

  • Over-the-Counter (OTC) Derivatives: Such as swaps, options, and forwards
  • Securities Financing Transactions: Including repos and securities lending
  • Long Settlement Transactions: Where settlement occurs after the standard settlement period
  • Margin Lending: Where loans are secured by financial securities

Counterparty Risk vs. Traditional Credit Risk

Key Counterparty Risk Concepts

Understanding counterparty credit risk requires familiarity with several key concepts:

  • Current Exposure: The current market value of a transaction if it were to be closed out immediately (also known as Mark-to-Market value).
  • Potential Future Exposure (PFE): The maximum expected exposure over a specified time horizon at a given confidence level.
  • Expected Exposure (EE): The average exposure at a future date, taking into account possible changes in market factors.
  • Credit Valuation Adjustment (CVA): The adjustment to the value of a derivative to account for the possibility of a counterparty's default.
  • Netting: The process of offsetting positive and negative exposures with the same counterparty to reduce overall exposure.
  • Collateral: Assets pledged to secure exposure, reducing the loss in case of default.

Counterparty Risk Regulatory Framework

Counterparty credit risk is regulated under various frameworks, including:

  • Basel III: Introduced the Standardized Approach for Counterparty Credit Risk (SA-CCR) and CVA capital charge.
  • Dodd-Frank Act: Requires central clearing for standardized OTC derivatives and margin requirements for non-cleared derivatives.
  • EMIR (European Market Infrastructure Regulation): Similar to Dodd-Frank, focuses on reducing systemic risk in OTC derivatives markets.

Exposure Modeling

Exposure modeling is the process of estimating the potential exposure to a counterparty over the life of a transaction or portfolio. This involves simulating future market scenarios to determine how the value of the transaction might evolve over time.

Key Exposure Metrics

  • Expected Exposure (EE): The average exposure at a future date, calculated as the probability-weighted average of positive exposures.
  • Potential Future Exposure (PFE): The maximum exposure at a future date with a high confidence level (typically 95% or 99%).
  • Expected Positive Exposure (EPE): The time-weighted average of expected exposures over a specified time horizon.
  • Effective Expected Positive Exposure (EEPE): A weighted average of effective EE, used in regulatory capital calculations.

Exposure Modeling Approaches

  • Monte Carlo Simulation: Generates thousands of possible future market scenarios to estimate exposure distribution.
  • Historical Simulation: Uses historical market data to project future exposures.
  • Parametric Methods: Uses statistical parameters and analytical formulas to estimate exposure.

Credit Valuation Adjustment (CVA) and Debt Valuation Adjustment (DVA)

Credit Valuation Adjustment (CVA) and Debt Valuation Adjustment (DVA) are accounting adjustments that reflect the fair value of counterparty credit risk in derivative transactions.

Credit Valuation Adjustment (CVA)

CVA is the adjustment to the value of a derivative to account for the possibility of a counterparty's default. It represents the market value of counterparty credit risk and is calculated as:

CVA = (1 - Recovery Rate) × ∑ Discount Factor × Expected Exposure × Probability of Default

Debt Valuation Adjustment (DVA)

DVA is the adjustment to the value of a derivative to account for the possibility of an institution's own default. It is essentially the CVA from the counterparty's perspective and represents a gain to the institution.

Funding Valuation Adjustment (FVA)

FVA accounts for the funding costs associated with uncollateralized derivatives. It reflects the cost of funding the uncollateralized portion of the derivative exposure.

Wrong-Way Risk

Wrong-way risk occurs when exposure to a counterparty is adversely correlated with the credit quality of that counterparty. In other words, the exposure increases as the counterparty's creditworthiness deteriorates.

Types of Wrong-Way Risk

  • Specific Wrong-Way Risk: Arises from a specific relationship between the counterparty and the underlying exposure (e.g., a bank having exposure to a company through a put option on the company's own stock).
  • General Wrong-Way Risk: Arises from a correlation between the counterparty's creditworthiness and general market risk factors (e.g., a bank in an emerging market with FX derivatives that would increase in value if the local currency depreciates).

Managing Wrong-Way Risk

  • Identification: Regular review of portfolios to identify potential wrong-way risk.
  • Measurement: Stress testing and scenario analysis to quantify the impact.
  • Mitigation: Setting exposure limits, requiring additional collateral, or restructuring transactions.
  • Monitoring: Ongoing surveillance of exposures and counterparty creditworthiness.

Regulatory Frameworks and Capital Requirements

Counterparty credit risk is subject to specific regulatory requirements for capital adequacy and risk management.

Standardized Approach for Counterparty Credit Risk (SA-CCR)

SA-CCR is the Basel Committee's standardized approach for measuring counterparty credit risk exposure. It replaced the Current Exposure Method (CEM) and Standardized Method (SM) with a more risk-sensitive approach that:

  • Differentiates between margined and non-margined transactions
  • Provides more meaningful recognition of netting benefits
  • Captures the risk of volatility in exposures through a risk factor-based approach
  • Includes a more detailed recognition of collateral

CVA Capital Charge

The CVA capital charge is a regulatory capital requirement for potential mark-to-market losses associated with a deterioration in counterparty creditworthiness. Banks can use either:

  • Standardized Approach: A formula-based approach that uses supervisory parameters.
  • Advanced Approach: Based on the bank's internal models for VaR calculation.

Central Clearing and Margin Requirements

Post-financial crisis regulations have focused on reducing systemic risk through:

  • Mandatory Central Clearing: Requiring standardized OTC derivatives to be cleared through central counterparties (CCPs).
  • Margin Requirements for Non-Cleared Derivatives: Requiring the exchange of initial and variation margin for non-cleared derivatives.
