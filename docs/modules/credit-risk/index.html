Introduction to Credit Risk

What is Credit Risk?

Credit risk is the risk of loss resulting from a borrower's failure to repay a loan or meet contractual obligations. It refers to the possibility that a borrower will default on their debt by failing to make required payments, which may be in the form of interest or principal repayments.

Credit risk is a critical concern for:

  • Banks and Financial Institutions: When lending to individuals and businesses
  • Investors: When purchasing bonds or debt securities
  • Businesses: When extending credit to customers
  • Governments: When issuing sovereign debt

Credit Risk Components

Key Credit Risk Concepts

Understanding credit risk requires familiarity with several key concepts:

  • Default: The failure of a borrower to make required payments as per the loan agreement.
  • Probability of Default (PD): The likelihood that a borrower will default on their obligations.
  • Loss Given Default (LGD): The amount of money a lender loses when a borrower defaults, usually expressed as a percentage of the total exposure.
  • Exposure at Default (EAD): The total value that a bank is exposed to when a borrower defaults.
  • Expected Loss (EL): The average credit loss that a lender can expect from a portfolio of loans, calculated as PD × LGD × EAD.

Credit Risk Regulatory Framework

Credit risk is regulated under various frameworks, including:

  • Basel Framework: The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision has established standards for credit risk capital requirements.
  • IFRS 9 / CECL: Accounting standards that require financial institutions to recognize expected credit losses.

Credit Scoring and Rating Methodologies

Credit scoring and rating methodologies are systematic approaches to assess the creditworthiness of borrowers. These methodologies help lenders make informed lending decisions and price loans appropriately.

Consumer Credit Scoring

Consumer credit scoring typically uses statistical models to predict the likelihood of default based on factors such as:

  • Payment History: Record of on-time payments
  • Credit Utilization: Amount of available credit being used
  • Length of Credit History: How long accounts have been open
  • Types of Credit: Mix of credit accounts (credit cards, loans, etc.)
  • New Credit: Recently opened accounts and credit inquiries

Corporate Credit Rating

Corporate credit ratings assess the creditworthiness of businesses and are typically provided by rating agencies like S&P, Moody's, and Fitch. These ratings consider factors such as:

  • Financial Performance: Profitability, cash flow, and leverage
  • Industry Outlook: Growth prospects and competitive dynamics
  • Management Quality: Track record and strategic direction
  • Business Position: Market share and competitive advantages
  • Country Risk: Political and economic stability

Expected Loss Calculation and Provisioning

Expected Loss (EL) is a key metric in credit risk management, representing the average loss a lender can expect from a portfolio of loans. It is calculated using the formula:

Expected Loss (EL) = Probability of Default (PD) × Loss Given Default (LGD) × Exposure at Default (EAD)

Under accounting standards like IFRS 9 and CECL, financial institutions are required to recognize expected credit losses and make provisions accordingly. These standards use a forward-looking approach that considers:

  • Past Events: Historical default and loss data
  • Current Conditions: Present economic environment
  • Future Forecasts: Projected economic scenarios

Credit Risk Capital Requirements

Banks are required to hold capital against credit risk in their portfolios. The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision has established frameworks for calculating credit risk capital requirements.

Standardized Approach

The Standardized Approach uses external credit ratings to determine risk weights for different types of exposures. Key features include:

  • Risk Weights: Assigned based on exposure type and external rating
  • Credit Risk Mitigation: Recognition of collateral, guarantees, and credit derivatives
  • Off-Balance Sheet Items: Conversion factors to determine exposure amount

Internal Ratings-Based (IRB) Approach

The IRB Approach allows banks to use their internal models to estimate credit risk parameters. There are two variants:

  • Foundation IRB (F-IRB): Banks estimate PD, but use regulatory values for LGD and EAD
  • Advanced IRB (A-IRB): Banks estimate all risk parameters (PD, LGD, EAD)

Credit Portfolio Management

Credit portfolio management involves optimizing the risk-return profile of a credit portfolio. Key aspects include:

  • Portfolio Diversification: Spreading exposure across different borrowers, industries, and geographies to reduce concentration risk.
  • Risk-Based Pricing: Setting loan terms and interest rates based on the borrower's risk profile.
  • Credit Derivatives: Using instruments like credit default swaps (CDS) to hedge credit risk.
  • Securitization: Pooling loans and issuing securities backed by the cash flows from these loans.
  • Economic Capital Allocation: Assigning capital to different business units based on their risk contribution.
